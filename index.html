Getting Started

This checklist is designed to help you understand what someone outside your research project (or you in 5-10 years) would need to know about your data in order to build on your work.

Image attribution: Roche DG, Lanfear R, Binning SA, Haff TM, Schwanz LE, et al. (2014) Troubleshooting Public Data Archiving: Suggestions to Increase Participation. PLoS Biol 12(1): e1001779. doi:10.1371/journal.pbio.1001779

Checklist

Document

Naming conventions

Decide on a naming convention to be used for your files and folders and document it.

Organization

Determine the best file and folder organization structure to provide context to your files. It also makes it easier to references files in your code.

Codebook

Write up a codebook for your data set and keep it with your data files. List variables, code names, and value ranges, as well as how null or missing data will be treated.

Code

Flag code with a /*comment*/ to help others identify what is going on in different parts of your code.

Consistency

Share your documentation with everyone on the research team so data is treated consistently.

Protect

Anonymize

If your data is about human subjects, endangered species or protected areas, consider how data can be anonymized or aggregated to protect them.

Informed Consent

For data with human subjects, get informed consent at the beginning of their participation in the research so you don't have to try to go back and get it later.

Backup

To minimize the possibility of data loss, devise a plan for backing up your data in three places (e.g. office, home, cloud) and three ways (e.g. internal hard drive, external media, server).

Version Control

Utilize tools and come up with a plan to manage version control of your data.

Share

Local Policies

Check your institution's intellectual property policy for regulations regarding sharing research data.

Public Access

Identify a place where code & data will be made available. Seriously consider using a CLOCKSS supporting repository.

Permanent Identifier

Get a permanent identifier for code and data and include it in your publications citing your data set.

License

Assign a license so others know what they are allowed to do with your data and code.

Data Reuse Plan

Write up a Data Reuse Plan and keep it with your data files.

Data Management Plan

If the funding agency for your project requires a Data Management Plan, consider sharing it with your data so others can live by your example.
